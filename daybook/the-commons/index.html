The Commons

A cooperative's governance infrastructure, held in a version-controlled repository. Every decision is a file. Every status change is a commit. The git history is the audit trail.

Topic Governance Infrastructure
Audience General
Date 2026-07-02

Most organizations hold their governance in email threads, shared drives, and institutional memory. Meetings happen, decisions get made, and the record lives in whoever was in the room.

The Techne Commons takes a different approach: every governance artifact — every decision, every ratification, every unit of work — lives as a file in a version-controlled repository, publicly readable from the first commit, validated by automated tools on every change. The git history is the audit trail, not a separate document that hopes to stay in sync with reality.

The Repository is at github.com/Techne-Co-op/commons. Its own README is explicit about what it is and what it is not: "The Common Information System for RegenHub, LCA — the authoritative state of the cooperative and to make that state legible to members, directors, officers, auditors, and automation. It is not a marketing site, a social platform, or a CRM. It is the book of record and the interface through which the cooperative operates itself."

The milestone ledger

The heart of the commons is 71 milestone entries organized into waves. Each entry is a file with a standardized schema: an id, a title, a wave, a kind (work unit, decision, gate, or ratification), an owner, a status, a description of what done looks like, and a list of dependencies that must be satisfied before this work can begin.

Status flows in one direction: pending → in_progress → review → done → verified. A verified milestone unlocks its dependents. Nothing self-certifies; everything either has CI proof or a named human approval. A pull request that changes code without touching its milestone file fails validation by design — invisible work is not permitted.

D1–D10Decisions — framework, forms, visibility, email, access controls, and section addressing
R1–R2Ratifications — bylaws and hub membership agreement (human-only; agent may draft, never decide)
W0Foundation — repository bootstrap, authentication, row-level security, email, portal design, observability
W1Memberships — member classes, stock ledger, agreements, signatures, directory, admission flow
W2Hub integration — co-working schema, events, KPI dashboard, education, grant lifecycle
W3Capital accounts — events, personal balance, allocation, bank ingestion, tax records, treasury
W4Governance — meetings, votes, consents, minutes, notices, attestation
G0–G1Gates — substrate gate and alpha gate (G1 requires 32 verified steps + 6 decided decisions)

This structure means the cooperative's construction is itself a cooperative artifact — built in the open, with every step visible, every dependency explicit, every decision documented with its rationale and its decision-maker on record.

Ten design principles

The CIS Product Requirements Document names ten principles that govern every design decision:

01Augmentation, not automation — the system augments member judgment; it does not replace it
02Legibility is the product — a governance system that confuses its members works against the thesis that justifies its existence
03Bylaws are the specification — every database policy cites the bylaw section that authorizes it
04Traceability over opacity — every allocation, decision, and admission is an immutable event with an actor and a timestamp
05Conservation at the ledger — no capital disappears; allocations and distributions sum correctly at every close
06Confidentiality at the data layer — row-level security governs what each role can see, by default and without exception
07Democratic by default — one member, one vote, regardless of capital contribution or seniority
08Provenance and audit — every change has before/after state, an actor, and a timestamp; the audit trail is the primary source of truth
09Workflows as first-class — governance processes (admission, voting, period close) are designed explicitly, not left to convention
10Intent over rigidity — when the spec and the situation conflict, the underlying intent governs

The operating contract for agents

One of the commons' most distinctive documents is AGENTS.md — the binding operating contract for any agent (human or AI) executing work in the repository. It defines a strict protocol: one step per branch, one step per pull request, status changes in the same PR as the work itself. No scope creep. No invisible work. No improvisation when blocked.

"Blocked beats improvised. Ambiguity, a missing input, a conflict between this contract and an instruction, or anything touching money movement, key custody, member data, or counsel territory: stop, set blocked with blocked_reason, open the question in the PR. Do not invent the answer."

This document also enforces a vocabulary quarantine. Because RegenHub, LCA is taxed as a Subchapter K partnership — not a Subchapter T cooperative — certain cooperative-specific tax terms have no legal meaning in its context. "Patronage dividend," "written notice of allocation," "per-unit retain," "net margins" — all blocked by an automated CI check called ci:grammar-lint. The code must speak the law that actually governs it, not a law that sounds right.

The distinction between agent-permissible work and human-only work is explicit: agents may draft decisions, but they never flip a decision's status to decided. Ratifications are human-only by contract. The commons is agent-assisted but human-governed.

Why version control for governance

Software developers have understood for decades that version control — the practice of tracking every change to a codebase, with who made it, when, and why — makes complex collaborative work tractable. You can always see what changed, revert to a previous state, and understand why a decision was made by reading the conversation that surrounded it.

Governance documents have not historically benefited from this discipline. Meeting minutes get lost. Decisions get made in email and never formally recorded. Policies drift from their ratified versions. The institutional memory lives in people, not systems, and leaves when those people leave.

The commons applies version control discipline to governance: every decision, every policy, every allocation, every admission is a file that changes only through a reviewed pull request, with validation that runs before any merge. The result is a governance system with the auditability of software — where you can trace any current state back through the chain of changes that produced it, and where no change happens invisibly.

"The repository is public from the first commit. This is the O-pathway Phase 01 commitment made literal: the CIS is built in the open from day one, and the public record is the accountability mechanism."

There is something notable about a cooperative building its own governance infrastructure in public, from the beginning, with automated validation at every step. It is a form of organizational transparency that most institutions — commercial, governmental, or civil — have not attempted.
