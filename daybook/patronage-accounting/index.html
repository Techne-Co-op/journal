Patronage Accounting

How cooperatives track contribution and distribute surplus — and why it requires more care than standard corporate accounting.

Topic Cooperative Finance
Audience General
Date 2026-07-02

In a corporation, profit flows to shareholders in proportion to their shares. Someone who owns 30% of the shares receives 30% of dividends, regardless of whether they did any work, brought any clients, or contributed any ideas.

Cooperatives work on a different principle. Surplus flows to members based on patronage — the economic activity they conducted with or for the cooperative during the period. The member who worked the most hours, generated the most revenue, and contributed the most capital doesn't necessarily receive the most in proportion to their investment. They receive it in proportion to their participation.

This isn't charity. It's an economic claim: the cooperative's value comes from what members do with and for it. Distributing surplus based on that activity is distributing it to its source.

The formula

Techne uses a four-factor weighted formula to calculate each member's patronage. The formula converts different kinds of contribution into a common unit, then calculates each member's share of the total.

Member Allocation = (Member Weighted Patronage / Total Weighted Patronage) × Net Surplus

Where Weighted Patronage = Σ (contribution_amount × category_weight) across all categories

The four categories, and their default weights in Techne's formula:

40%
Labor

Hours worked, converted to dollars at defensible market rates ($50–$150/hr depending on role)

30%
Revenue

Direct income generated for the cooperative — clients brought, contracts delivered

20%
Capital

Financial capital contributed — cash, equipment, property at fair market value

10%
Community

Relational work — governance participation, mentorship, institutional memory, culture

The weighting expresses cooperative values in arithmetic form. A 40:20 labor-to-capital split says: an hour of work matters twice as much as an equivalent dollar invested. Different cooperatives with different economics might weight differently — a capital-intensive production cooperative might weight capital more heavily. The formula is the policy made computable.

Capital accounts and IRC 704(b)

Every member has a capital account — a running ledger of their economic stake in the entity. It tracks four things: contributions in (cash, property), allocations in (share of surplus), distributions out (cash paid to member), and losses out (share of any losses). The ending balance is what the member would theoretically receive if the cooperative liquidated.

For these capital accounts to be legally valid under federal tax law, they must satisfy a test called "substantial economic effect" under IRC Section 704(b). This test has three conditions: the accounts must be maintained correctly, liquidating distributions must follow account balances, and the accounts must not go negative in ways that aren't accounted for.

The reason this matters: if the accounts fail the test, the IRS can recharacterize the allocations as it sees fit, potentially assigning income to members who didn't receive cash distributions in that year. Valid accounts protect members from this outcome.

"The cooperative intends to maintain capital accounts for patron members in accordance with Treasury Regulation § 1.704-1(b)(2)(iv) ... such allocations are intended to have substantial economic effect within the meaning of IRC § 704(b)."

The dual-basis problem

Here is where cooperative accounting gets technically demanding. Every member has not one capital account, but two: a book capital account (tracking fair market value) and a tax capital account (tracking tax basis). These diverge whenever property is contributed at a value different from its cost basis.

If Alice contributes equipment worth $10,000 on the market but which cost her $7,000, the cooperative must track both values simultaneously. The $3,000 difference is called a "built-in gain" — a layer that belongs to Alice. If the cooperative later sells that equipment for $12,000, the first $3,000 of tax gain must be allocated to Alice, even if the rest is split among all members per the patronage formula. This is an IRC Section 704(c) allocation.

Running two ledgers in parallel, tracking which contributions carry built-in gains, and calculating special allocations correctly — this is the technical heart of partnership tax compliance. It is not optional, and it cannot be approximated.

Three layers

Techne's patronage accounting system is built in three layers, each distinct in purpose:

Layer 1 — Patronage Accounting (Policy)

The operating agreement and bylaws define what patronage means, how contributions are categorized, how weights are set, and how allocations are calculated. This is the law of the cooperative — binding on all members and agents.

Layer 2 — Composable Tools (Implementation)

Three integrated tools implement the policy. Treasury handles the chart of accounts and transaction recording. People handles contribution logging, valuation, and member profiles. Agreements encodes the formula, runs the allocation calculation, and orchestrates the period close. Each tool is separate and communicates via events; none requires the others to function.

Layer 3 — Cloud Credits (Economic Unit)

Service credits (CLOUD) serve as the medium of account within the cooperative. One CLOUD equals ten cents of real compute capacity. Patronage allocations, infrastructure costs, and engineer billing all denominate in CLOUD. This creates a single legible unit for all economic flows, without speculation or complexity.

The architecture is built on REA ontology — Resource, Event, Agent. Every economic action is an Event, performed by an Agent, affecting a Resource. The ledger is never updated directly; instead, events are appended, and current state is derived by replaying them. This makes the audit trail identical to the operational database — there is one source of truth, and it cannot be silently altered.

What members experience

At the end of each period, every member receives an allocation statement: their contributions by category, the weighted calculation, their share of the surplus, and the split between cash distributed and capital retained. They also receive a Schedule K-1 — the federal tax document that reports their distributive share of the cooperative's income for the year.

A critical point about K-1 timing: members are taxed on their allocation in the year it is earned, whether or not they received cash. If the cooperative retains a portion of the allocation in the member's capital account — a common practice that allows the cooperative to retain working capital — the member still owes tax on that amount in the current year. The membership agreement requires explicit consent to this treatment, so members understand the obligation before joining.

The system aims to make all of this visible in real time: running contribution totals, projected allocations, capital account balances updated as contributions are approved. The principle throughout is legibility — a member should be able to trace any number in their statement back to the events that produced it, without asking anyone.
