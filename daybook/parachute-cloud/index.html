Parachute Cloud

A vault for your work, held in common — and what that actually means.

Topic Memory Infrastructure
Audience General
Date 2026-07-02

The word vault carries a meaning that "cloud storage" doesn't. A vault is built to hold something precious. It has a door. You decide who has a key.

Parachute Cloud is a knowledge infrastructure system built around that distinction. Its tagline is direct: "A vault for your work. Held in common." Three words do significant work there. Vault implies security and intentionality. Work means the actual substance of what you make — not files, not documents, work. Held in common means the vault belongs to a group, not to a platform company.

It launched its first public release in July 2026, starting at three dollars. That number is not incidental. It signals something: this is priced like a physical service (a stamp, a tool rental), not like a software subscription designed to grow without bound. Three dollars buys thirty cloud credits. One credit equals ten cents of actual compute capacity — not a fictional unit, but a prepaid claim on real hardware.

The Daybook

Every Parachute vault has a Daybook as its primary surface. The metaphor is deliberate. A daybook is what merchants and craftspeople kept before accounting software existed — a running record of what happened, in order, as it happened. Not categorized. Not optimized. Just witnessed.

In Parachute, the Daybook has a dual-layer structure. Private entries are yours alone — visible only to you until you choose otherwise. Public entries belong to your group — shared as part of the collective record the moment you mark them so. The system enforces this distinction cleanly: there is no accidental publishing, no default visibility you have to opt out of.

"To keep your own reflections, you need a place to keep them. This opens a private daybook in your name. It does not make you a member of the cooperative, and it grants no vote and no account. It gives you one thing: a daybook only you can read, until you choose otherwise."

This design reflects a specific philosophy: the group's knowledge commons should be built from individual acts of voluntary contribution, not from surveillance of private thought. You share what you choose to share. The rest stays yours.

What local-first means here

Most cloud storage works by sending your data to servers owned by a company, then letting you access it through that company's interface. Your data persists as long as the company does, and as long as you keep paying, and as long as the company decides to serve your geography and comply with local law.

Parachute is built differently. The core software is AGPL-licensed — which means anyone can read it, run it, and modify it. Data lives in SQLite, a file format that runs on your machine without requiring a database server. The hosted cloud offering wraps the same code in a convenient service, but the data and the logic are not locked to that service.

This matters because it changes the power relationship. The hosted service is a convenience, not a dependency. If the company disappeared tomorrow, a member with technical knowledge could run the same software from a laptop. The hosted offering earns your use each month; it doesn't hold your data hostage to compel it.

The three-part architecture

Parachute is organized as a hub of composable services. Each service handles one concern and speaks a shared protocol — a module contract — that lets them discover each other and wire up without a central registry. The core services are:

parachute-vault

The knowledge graph — notes, tags, links, attachments in SQLite. The foundation everything else connects to.

parachute-lens

The browser UI — a progressive web app that can run anywhere, reads and writes to any vault via the standard API.

parachute-cli

The local coordinator — installs, starts, stops, and routes between services. Handles Tailscale for private networking.

Beyond the core, the protocol allows additional modules: audio transcription (parachute-scribe), text-to-speech (parachute-narrate), messaging gateway (parachute-channel), and an agent framework (parachute-agents) that lets AI agents read from and write to the vault with their own scoped credentials.

This last module is where Parachute diverges most sharply from earlier knowledge tools. Traditional note-taking apps were designed for human readers and writers. Parachute is designed for humans and AI agents equally — both are first-class participants with explicit permissions, both write immutable entries, both can be audited.

Cloud credits and the postage stamp model

The metaphor that explains Parachute's economics is the postage stamp. When you buy a stamp, you're not purchasing access to a network — you're prepaying for a specific physical service: the delivery of one letter. The post office can issue stamps because it has trucks, workers, and routes. The stamp is a claim on that capacity.

Parachute's cloud credits work the same way. One credit costs ten cents. That ten cents represents a real quantity of compute: CPU cycles, memory, storage, and bandwidth on actual hardware. The cooperative that issues these credits has the hardware; the credits are prepaid claims on its use.

Why cooperative law, not corporate law

Parachute Cloud is a product of Open Parachute, PBC — a Colorado Public Benefit Corporation — operating alongside RegenHub, LCA, a Colorado Limited Cooperative Association. The legal structure is not incidental to the product; it's constitutive of it.

In a standard software company, the company owns your data while it's on their servers. The terms of service define what they can do with it. If the company is acquired, the acquirer inherits those terms and can revise them. The relationship is asymmetric: the company has leverage, the user has consent flows.

Cooperative law changes this. The LCA structure means the infrastructure is owned and governed by its members — the people who use it, not the investors who capitalized it. One member, one vote, regardless of how much you've contributed financially. Patronage distributions go to members based on their participation, not their shares. The cooperative's surplus serves its members, not its stockholders, because there are no stockholders.

The same capability owned by shareholders replaces. Owned by members, it amplifies. The technology is the same. The institution is the variable.

Parachute as design partner

There's a less obvious reason why a cooperative made sense as the first user of enterprise knowledge infrastructure. Cooperatives concentrate requirements that would normally be spread across many different enterprise contexts.

A cooperative needs multi-member identity (who is a member, with what role?). It needs governance records (what was voted on, by whom, when?). It needs financial transparency (how are surpluses allocated?). It needs agent credentialing (Nou, the cooperative's AI agent, needs its own vault access with scoped permissions). It needs audit trails that survive governance disputes. It needs data portability in case the cooperative reorganizes.

Designing for a cooperative is designing for organizational complexity in a small, high-trust, high-accountability context. The requirements that emerge translate directly to what large organizations need — but they surface in a setting where the tradeoffs are visible and the stakes are legible.

This is why RegenHub, LCA serves as the design partner for Parachute Enterprise. Not because it's a typical customer, but because it's a compressed version of every difficult customer — one that must actually govern itself, account for itself, and survive without any single point of authority.
